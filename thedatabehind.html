Data worth exploring

datasets I find myself going back to

Instacart Market Basket Dataset
3M+ grocery orders. Reorder patterns tell you more about how someone actually lives than almost any survey — people's habits show up in their carts before they'd ever describe them out loud.
RETAIL · CUSTOMER BEHAVIOR
Statistics Canada — Open Data
CPI, labour force surveys, consumer spending patterns. Real Canada, real decisions. Most analysts don't dig into this as much as they should.
ECONOMICS · PUBLIC DATA
Kaggle Credit Card Fraud Dataset
Class imbalance at its worst — 0.17% fraud rate. The real challenge is always defining what "normal" looks like before you can see what isn't. This one teaches you that fast.
FINANCIAL CRIME · ML
Lyft / Uber Open Datasets
Ride routing, demand patterns, and surge pricing data. I'm fascinated by how platforms like Lyft optimize for thousands of simultaneous decisions — pricing, routing, driver allocation — in real time.

Questions I keep circling back to

click any question to expand

  • When someone enters a new market, what does their first week of behavior actually tell you?
    customer acquisition · onboarding analytics · cohort behavior
    +
    The first few transactions a customer makes are some of the most information-dense data a company has. Are they exploring cautiously or diving straight in? What category do they hit first? Do they return in 3 days or 3 weeks? That behavior predicts long-term retention far better than any demographic signal. I find myself thinking about this whenever I look at customer acquisition data — the pattern is usually already there in week one, we just don't always look.
  • How does a company like Lyft or Skip decide that one route is worth more than another in real time?
    dynamic pricing · route optimization · operations analytics
    +
    There's a decision being made in milliseconds that balances driver availability, demand density, time windows, and price sensitivity — all at once, for thousands of trips simultaneously. That's not just optimization, it's a miniature market being created and resolved in real time. I've been digging into how surge pricing and routing algorithms interact and what the tradeoffs actually look like at scale.
  • Why does fraud look like normal behavior — until it doesn't?
    AML · anomaly detection · behavioral baselines
    +
    The hardest part of fraud detection isn't finding the outlier — it's defining what normal looks like in the first place. A customer who suddenly makes 12 transactions in a day might be a fraudster or might have just gotten paid and gone Christmas shopping. Context is everything, and most rule-based systems can't hold context across time. This is why I built a z-score and velocity based system for my fraud detection project — you have to establish baselines before any threshold makes sense.
  • What does a customer's shopping cart actually reveal about how they're feeling that week?
    retail analytics · basket analysis · implicit behavioral signals
    +
    Convenience food, comfort snacks, late-night orders — all of these are behavioral signals that show up before a customer would ever describe themselves as "stressed" or "time-crunched." The Instacart dataset is full of this kind of implicit signal. I've spent a lot of time on it because I think customer understanding that comes from observed behavior is fundamentally more honest than what comes from surveys or focus groups.
  • At what point does a dashboard stop helping people decide and start making them anxious?
    BI design · information density · decision support
    +
    I've seen dashboards with 30 KPIs on a single screen that technically had all the right data — and led to worse decisions than a simple weekly summary email would have. There's a real cognitive cost to putting too much in front of someone. The dashboards that work are the ones that answer one specific question clearly, not the ones that try to show everything. I think about this every time I start designing a report.
